PMYabor Package

Description

The purpose of this vignette is to provide some insight and direction in using the PMYabor package. This package consists of five functions which are used in a paper on partially matched samples by Pei Fen Kuan and Bo Huang1(https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3717400/).

Partially matched samples occur when one’s dataset, \(x\) and \(y\), have some missing values. The purpose of the paper is to provide a simple and robust method for handling these missing values in statistical analyses. The paper focused on microarrays and next generation sequencing as ways to demonstrate the efficiency of the method outlined.

The five procedures outlined in the paper are the following:

  1. Liptak’s weighted Z-test
  2. Kim et al’s modified t-statistic
  3. Looney and Jones’s corrected Z-test
  4. Lin and Stivers’s MLE based test under heteroscedasticity
  5. Ehbohm’s MLE based test under homoscedasticity

Functions and Setup

library(PMYabor)

Liptak’s weighted Z-test

Arguments

  • \(x\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • \(y\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • alternative.pool a character string specifying the alternative hypothesis for the weighted Z-test, must be one of “greater” (default), “less”, or “two.sided”
  • alternative.t a character string specifying the alternative hypothesis in the event of a standard two sample t-test, must be one of “two.sided” (default), “greater” or “less”

Usage

liptak.z(x,y,alternative.pool='greater',alternative.t='two.sided')

Formulas

\[ \begin{aligned} p_{ci}=1-\Phi\Bigg(\frac{w_1Z_{1i}+w_2Z_{2i}}{\sqrt{w_1^2+w_2^2}}\Bigg) \end{aligned} \] where \[ \begin{aligned} Z_{ai}=\Phi^{-1}(1-p_{ai}) \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} a=1,2 \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} w_1=\sqrt{2n_1} \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} w_2=\sqrt{n_2+n_3} \end{aligned} \] Further, the two sided p-value is obtained by \[ \begin{aligned} p_{ci}^*=\begin{cases} 2p_{ci} & p_{ci}<1/2 \\ 2(1-p_{ci}) & otherwise \end{cases} \end{aligned} \]

Examples

x <- c(120,212,NA,300,323,NA,NA,96,178,164)
y <- c(NA,153,304,177,NA,90,89,200,110,40)
liptak.z(x,y,alternative.pool = "greater")
#> $test
#> [1] "weighted Z-test"
#> 
#> $z1i
#> [1] 1.113677
#> 
#> $z2i
#> [1] 0.4201559
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.124683
liptak.z(x,y,alternative.pool = "less")
#> $test
#> [1] "weighted Z-test"
#> 
#> $z1i
#> [1] -1.113677
#> 
#> $z2i
#> [1] -0.4201559
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.875317
liptak.z(x,y,alternative.pool = "two.sided")
#> [[1]]
#> [1] "two.sided weighted Z-test"
#> 
#> $z1i
#> [1] 0.6267314
#> 
#> $z2i
#> [1] -0.4520169
#> 
#> $pc
#> [1] 0.4010029
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.8020059

Kim et al’s modified t-statistic

Arguments

  • \(x\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • \(y\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • alternative a character string specifying the alternative hypothesis, must be one of “two.sided” (default), “greater” or “less”

Usage

kim.t(x,y,alternative = 'two.sided')

Formulas

\[ \begin{aligned} t_3=\frac{{n_1}\bar{D}+{n_H}(\bar{T}-\bar{N})}{\sqrt{{n_1}{S_D}^2+{n_H}^2(S_N^2/n_3+S_T^2/n_2)}} \end{aligned} \] where \(\bar{D}\) is the mean difference of the \(n_1\) paired samples, \(\bar{T}\) and \(\bar{N}\) are the means for the \(n_2\) and \(n_3\) unmatched samples, respectively. \(S_D\), \(S_T\) and \(S_N\) are the corresponding sample standard deviations, and \(n_H\) is the harmonic mean of \(n_3\) and \(n_3\). The null distribution of \(t_3\) is approximated with a standard Gaussian distribution.

Note that \[ \begin{aligned} n_H=\frac{2}{1/n_2+1/n_3} \end{aligned} \]

Examples

x <- c(120,212,NA,300,323,NA,NA,96,178,164)
y <- c(NA,153,304,177,NA,90,89,200,110,40)
kim.t(x,y,alternative = "two.sided")
#> $t3
#> [1] 1.141308
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.2537419
kim.t(x,y,alternative = "greater")
#> $t3
#> [1] 1.141308
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.1268709
kim.t(x,y,alternative = "less")
#> $t3
#> [1] 1.141308
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.8731291

Looney and Jones’s corrected Z-test

Arguments

  • \(x\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • \(y\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • alternative a character string specifying the alternative hypothesis, must be one of “two.sided” (default), “greater” or “less”

Usage

z.corrected(x,y,alternative = 'two.sided')

Formulas

\[ \begin{aligned} Z_{corr}=\frac{\bar{T^*}-\bar{N^*}}{\sqrt{S_T^{*2}/(n_1+n_2)+S_N^{*2}/(n_1+n_3)-2n_1S_{TN_1}/(n_1+n_2)(n_1+n_3)}} \end{aligned} \] where \(\bar{T^*}\) and \(\bar{N^*}\) are the means for the \(n_1+n_2\) and \(n_1+n_3\) matched and unmatched samples combined, respectively. \(S_T^{*}\) and \(S_N^{*}\) are the corresponding sample standard deviations, and \(S_{TN_1}\) is the sample covariance of the \(n_1\) paired samples. \(Z_{corr}\) reduces to a paired sample or two-sample Z-test when \(n_2=n_3=0\) or \(n_1\), respectively.

Note that \[ \begin{aligned} S_{TN_1}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n_1}(T_i-\bar{T_1})(N_i-\bar{N_1})}{(n_1-1)} \end{aligned} \]

Examples

x <- c(120,212,NA,300,323,NA,NA,96,178,164)
y <- c(NA,153,304,177,NA,90,89,200,110,40)
z.corrected(x,y,alternative = "two.sided")
#> $z.stat
#> [1] 1.252635
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.2103387
z.corrected(x,y,alternative = "greater")
#> $z.stat
#> [1] 1.252635
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.1051694
z.corrected(x,y,alternative = "less")
#> $z.stat
#> [1] 1.252635
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.8948306

Lin and Stivers’s MLE based test under heteroscedasticity

Arguments

  • \(x\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • \(y\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • alternative a character string specifying the alternative hypothesis, must be one of “two.sided” (default), “greater” or “less”

Usage

MLE.het(x,y,alternative = 'two.sided')

Formulas

\[ \begin{aligned} Z_{LS}=\frac{\{f(\bar{T_1}-\bar{T})-g(\bar{N_1}-\bar{N})+\bar{T}-\bar{N}\}}{\sqrt{V_1}} \end{aligned} \] where \[ \begin{aligned} r=S_{TN_1}/S_{T_1}S_{N_1} \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} f=n_1(n_1+n_3+n_2S_{TN_1}/S_{T_1}^2)\{(n_1+n_2)(n_1+n_3)-n_2n_3r^2\}^{-1} \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} g=n_1(n_1+n_2+n_3S_{TN_1}/S_{N_1}^2)\{(n_1+n_2)(n_1+n_3)-n_2n_3r^2\}^{-1} \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} V_1=\frac{\{f^2/n_1+(1-f)^2/n_2\}S_{T_1}^2(n_1-1)+\{g^2/n_1+(1-g)^2/n_3\}S_{N_1}^2(n_1-1)-2fgS_{TN_1}(n_1-1)/n_1}{(n_1-1)} \end{aligned} \] \(\bar{T_1}\) and \(\bar{N_1}\) are the means for the n1 paired samples. \(S_{T1}\) and \(S_{N1}\) are the corresponding sample standard deviations, respectively. Further, \(Z_{LS}\) can be approximated by a \(t\) distribution with \(n_1\) degrees of freedom.

Examples

x <- c(120,212,NA,300,323,NA,NA,96,178,164)
y <- c(NA,153,304,177,NA,90,89,200,110,40)
MLE.het(x,y,alternative = "two.sided")
#> $Z.LS
#> [1] 1.545846
#> 
#> $df
#> [1] 5
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.1828063
MLE.het(x,y,alternative = "greater")
#> $Z.LS
#> [1] 1.545846
#> 
#> $df
#> [1] 5
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.09140317
MLE.het(x,y,alternative = "less")
#> $Z.LS
#> [1] 1.545846
#> 
#> $df
#> [1] 5
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.9085968

Ehbohm’s MLE based test under homoscedasticity

Arguments

  • \(x\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • \(y\) a (non-empty) numeric vector of data values
  • alternative a character string specifying the alternative hypothesis, must be one of “two.sided” (default), “greater” or “less”

Usage

MLE.hom(x,y,alternative = 'two.sided')

Formulas

\[ \begin{aligned} Z_E=\frac{\{f^*(\bar{T_1}-\bar{T})-g^*(\bar{N_1}-\bar{N})+\bar{T}-\bar{N}\}}{\sqrt{V_1^*}} \end{aligned} \] where \[ \begin{aligned} r=S_{TN_1}/S_{T_1}S_{N_1} \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} f^*=n_1(n_1+n_3+n_2r)\{(n_1+n_2)(n_1+n_3)-n_2n_3r^2\}^{-1} \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} g^*=n_1(n_1+n_2+n_3r)\{(n_1+n_2)(n_1+n_3)-n_2n_3r^2\}^{-1} \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} \hat{\sigma}^2=\frac{S_{T_1}^2(n_1-1)+S_{N_1}^2(n_1-1)+(1+r^2)[S_T^2(n_2-1)+S_{N_1}^2(n_3-1)]}{2(n_1-1)+(1+r^2)(n_2+n_3-2)} \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} V_1^*=\hat{\sigma}^2\Bigg\{\frac{2n_1(1-r)+(n_2+n_3)(1-r^2)}{(n_1+n_2)(n_1+n_3)-n_2n_3r^2}\Bigg\} \end{aligned} \] \(Z_E\) can be approximated by a \(t\) distribution with \(n_1\) degrees of freedom.

Examples

x <- c(120,212,NA,300,323,NA,NA,96,178,164)
y <- c(NA,153,304,177,NA,90,89,200,110,40)
MLE.hom(x,y,alternative = "two.sided")
#> $Z.E
#> [1] 1.186233
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.2888204
MLE.hom(x,y,alternative = "greater")
#> $Z.E
#> [1] 1.186233
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.1444102
MLE.hom(x,y,alternative = "less")
#> $Z.E
#> [1] 1.186233
#> 
#> $p.value
#> [1] 0.8555898
